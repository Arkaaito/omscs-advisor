CONGRATULATIONS!

Welcome to the OMSCS.

OMSCS is a very unusual (very unusual) Master's program. This is an unofficial collection of clutter site aimed at helping you learn your way around.

One of the first things you'll notice about OMSCS is that it's big. (Within a few short years we are expected to account for 8% of all CS MS graduates in the US.)

Another thing you'll quickly pick up is that there's a huge range of student professions and domains. There are countless web devs, countless mobile devs, countless professional educators, countless musicians, countless anything, really.

These two points together combine to yield an interesting phenomenon: OMSCS is very student-driven. Outside of the classes themselves, most of the really useful tools in the program are thrown together by some student who went, "Man, why don't we even have a simple X for Y yet?"

This page doesn't quite rise to that level. However, it hopes to introduce you to many of those tools as you go into your first semester. If you find it useful, bookmark it and come back to it now and again, as I'll try to keep it up to date; if you think it could be better, please (please!) suggest improvements and/or open a pull request on Github.

FAQ

When will I get my Georgia Tech e-mail address?

When it's ready.

But wait, all the OMSCS student tools require a GATech e-mail! So what do I do until then?

Well, the good news is, obscure workarounds will allow you to sidestep this requirement in most instances if you really want to begin preparing for your classes early. Here are some ideas:

  • Decide what class you'll be taking first by browsing course info on OMSCS Official and course reviews on OMS Central.
  • Ask to be added to the Slack group if you're into that sort of thing. If you enjoy the combination of mildly helpful information and snark found on this site you'll probably like the Slack group, too. Self-signup for Slack requires a GATech e-mail address, but you can e-mail me (my GATech e-mail alias is "smiley") to be added manually.
  • Ask to be added to the Google+ community. Just don't follow up by posting a bunch of questions about things that are already in the FAQ, or much kvetching will ensure. Speaking of which...
  • Read the FAQ. Smarter people than me have spent more time than I have putting together a comprehensive list of answers to general questions about the program and its logistics.
  • While you're reading things, consider reading this Reddit post with suggestions about preparation. It has suggestions for specific courses but also more general ideas for how to make up background you may be lacking. Particularly valuable if your undergraduate degree wasn't in CS and/or you don't work in software development now.
  • Watch the OMSCS orientation. You can't access the course on Udacity until you get your GATech account but the videos are posted publicly on YouTube and indexed here for your convenience (given a loose definition of "convenience").
  • Depending on what your first course is, the lectures are likely accessible through the public-facing Udacity site. All publicly-available GATech courses are listed here.
  • If the videos aren't yet available publicly, you can still get the video materials (not the quizzes, though) from YouTube directly. Some links are indexed on this site (check out the sidebar here).

Any other "common knowledge" not covered in the FAQ or the orientation?

Hmm. A few things come to mind:

  • In this program, you'll have classmates who are famous and at the top of their fields. You'll also have classmates who have no idea what they're doing and no ability or willingness to learn. However, most of us will fall into the vast middle: we may have no idea what we're doing yet but we're likely to get there eventually.
  • In the good/bad old days, new courses were highly unpredictable. (Look at the oldest reviews for Computer Networks [CS6250] and CCA [CS6505], if you want to get a feel for this.) The program is maturing now so there's a bit more consistency, but it's still safest to treat courses as "beta" for their first couple of terms. The professor is usually inexperienced with the OMSCS format so responses to questions may be extremely delayed and Piazza may spontaneously combust; difficulty may be wildly high or low; grades may take weeks to come back because they realize they forgot to write any grading code before the assignment deadline; etc., etc., etc. The corollary to this is that you probably don't want to take a totally new course your first term, or even one that's new enough to not have its videos posted on the public Udacity site yet. It's easier (and less stressful!) to weather these vagaries once you have a few terms under your belt.
  • Taking CCA your first term is a YOLO move. 'Nuff said.
